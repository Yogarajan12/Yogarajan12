**Yogarajan Sivakumar** — Machine Learning Researcher in Interpretable and Uncertainty-Aware AI

Yogarajan Sivakumar is a machine learning researcher working at the intersection of interpretable, uncertainty-aware artificial intelligence and its application to clinical and biomedical problems, with a growing focus on mechanistic interpretability and AI safety. He holds an MS in Computer Engineering with an AI specialization from Stevens Institute of Technology (2026) and a BEng in Electrical Engineering from the National University of Singapore (2024).

His research is guided by a conviction formed while working alongside physicians on real clinical systems: models that make predictions and prescriptions are only trustworthy when they can explain their reasoning and honestly quantify their own uncertainty. That principle runs through all of his work in explainable AI (XAI), mechanistic interpretability, uncertainty quantification, conformal prediction, deep learning, and AI safety for medical and biomedical machine learning.

Yogarajan Sivakumar has led and contributed to a range of projects. NeuroState is his gradient-isolated boundary-learning method for interpretable EEG transformers, pairing strong performance across the Sleep-EDF, CHB-MIT, and TUAB datasets with concept-level interpretability and sub-second seizure onset localization. His medical imaging work includes a quality-gated fetal ultrasound classifier built on a ResNet-18 backbone with diffusion-based reconstruction uncertainty, reaching 95.50 percent overall accuracy and 99.14 percent on non-deferred cases. In clinical operations, he built a calibrated no-show and overbooking prediction system for outpatient clinics, projected to reduce scheduling costs substantially. In computational oncology, he developed a graph neural network for cancer drug response prediction with split conformal prediction for calibrated uncertainty, achieving a Pearson correlation of 0.909 on GDSC1. He has also worked on multivariate gait analysis using random forests, hidden Markov models, LSTMs, and spatio-temporal graph convolutional networks, and has contributed experimentally to work on efficient reinforcement learning from human feedback and on game-theoretic evaluation of large language models.

He has recently been actively engaged with the mechanistic interpretability and AI safety research community, and his broader research experience includes collaboration with physicians at Singapore General Hospital on ICU outcome prediction and an industry machine learning role at Infineon Technologies.
